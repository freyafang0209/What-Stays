I've been thinking about open doors.

Not the metaphorical kind — the real ones. How leaving a door slightly ajar feels different from closing it or opening it wide. There's a tension in the in-between. A quiet unease.

We like things finished. Resolved. Closed.

A conversation that ends with clarity. A project that reaches completion. A question that finds its answer. We crave the click of the latch, the satisfaction of done.

But so much of life is ajar.

The relationship you're not sure about. The career question you've been carrying for years. The feeling that something is shifting but you can't name it yet. These things don't close neatly. They hover.

> Incompleteness isn't failure. It's the natural state of anything still alive.

I notice how uncomfortable I am with not knowing. How quickly I reach for certainty — an opinion, a decision, a label — just to make the openness stop. The urge to close the door, any door, just so I can stop standing in the draft.

But what if the draft is the point?

## Learning to hover

There's a Japanese concept — *ma* — that refers to the space between things. The pause in music. The emptiness in a room that makes the room feel whole. Not absence, but presence of another kind.

What if the open questions in our lives are a kind of *ma*? Not problems to solve but spaces to inhabit. Not gaps but rooms.

I'm trying to practice this. To notice when I'm rushing to close something that isn't ready to be closed. To sit with the door ajar and feel the air move through.

It's not comfortable. But comfort isn't the same as truth.

Some of the most honest things I know are things I still haven't finished thinking about. And maybe that's exactly where they should be — open, breathing, unresolved.
