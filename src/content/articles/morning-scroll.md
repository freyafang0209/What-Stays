The first thing I do every morning is reach for my phone. Not to call anyone. Not because something is urgent. Just because my hand knows the way.

It happens before thought. Before I've felt the sheets or noticed the light. Before I've remembered what day it is or what I dreamt about. My thumb already knows the gesture — the unlock, the pull-down, the scroll.

By the time I'm fully awake, I've already been somewhere else.

Someone's outrage. Someone's announcement. Someone's perfect morning in a place more beautiful than my bedroom. The world rushes in before I've had a chance to find my own edges.

I wonder sometimes what those first few minutes used to hold. Before the phone, before the scroll. Did people lie still and listen to birds? Did they stare at the ceiling and let thoughts arrive slowly, like guests rather than intruders?

> The morning is the one time we get to begin. And we hand it away before we know we've done it.

I've tried to stop. I've set the phone across the room. I've turned off notifications. But the pull isn't about notifications — it's about the discomfort of being alone with yourself in the quiet.

The scroll fills a silence we didn't even know we were afraid of.

## A different first minute

Last Tuesday I didn't reach for it. Not out of discipline — I just forgot. I lay there for a while, watching light move across the wall. I thought about my mother. I noticed I was hungry.

Nothing happened. And everything happened.

The day had a different texture. Slower, like it had been given back to me instead of taken from the start.

I'm not going to pretend I've stopped scrolling. I haven't. But now I notice the moment my hand reaches — and sometimes, just sometimes, I let it rest there instead.

That pause is worth more than anything on the screen.
