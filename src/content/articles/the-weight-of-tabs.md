I have forty-three tabs open right now. I counted.

Some of them are articles I saved weeks ago — things I promised myself I'd read when I had time. A recipe for something with lentils. A long essay about solitude that I found through someone's story, then forgot. A flight search for a trip I haven't decided to take.

Each tab is a tiny commitment I made to my future self. A small act of faith that later, things would be quieter. That I'd have a slow Sunday, a clear afternoon, a stretch of uninterrupted hours.

But those hours never arrive on their own. They don't knock politely at the door and wait.

> We keep things open because closing them feels like giving up on a version of ourselves that has time.

There is a particular kind of weight that comes from unfinished things. Not heavy, exactly — more like a low hum. A background awareness that somewhere, something is waiting. That you are, in some small way, behind.

I think about the tabs I'll never read. The ones that have been open so long their titles have faded into the browser bar like old spines on a shelf. I don't remember why I saved them. I just know that closing them feels like loss.

But what is it that I'm losing?

Not the information — that lives on, somewhere, forever retrievable. What I'm losing is the fantasy. The imagined self who reads every article, cooks every recipe, books every trip. The self who is never behind, never overwhelmed, always caught up.

That self doesn't exist. She never did.

## What stays

Maybe the practice isn't about catching up. Maybe it's about letting things fall away — gently, without guilt — and noticing what remains.

The things that stay open in your mind without effort. The questions that keep returning even when you close every tab. The curiosity that doesn't need a bookmark.

I closed thirty of them this morning. It felt like setting something down.

Not everything that asks for our attention deserves it. And not everything we release is lost.

Some things just needed to be noticed on their way through.
