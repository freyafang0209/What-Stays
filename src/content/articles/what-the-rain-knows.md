It rained all day yesterday. Not dramatically — no thunder, no spectacle. Just a steady, quiet rain that turned the windows into something worth watching.

I sat with it for a while. Not doing anything with the rain. Just being near it.

There's something that happens when you listen to rain without trying to make it productive. Without putting on a rain sounds playlist or using it as background for focus. Just listening, the way you might listen to someone you love who isn't saying anything important.

The rain doesn't hurry. It doesn't have a schedule. It falls at exactly the speed it falls, and that is enough.

> We've built a world that runs on urgency. Rain is the opposite of urgency. Maybe that's why it calms us — it reminds us that not everything needs to be fast.

I think about pace a lot. How everything around me seems to be accelerating — conversations, decisions, the space between question and answer. How even rest has become optimized. Sleep trackers. Recovery metrics. The quantified pause.

The rain doesn't measure itself. It doesn't know how many millimeters it's produced or whether today's rainfall was better than yesterday's. It just falls.

## The speed of things

There's a kind of knowing that only arrives slowly. The insight that comes after a walk, not during it. The understanding that settles in overnight. The answer that appears only after you've stopped asking the question.

We've made a world that doesn't trust slowness. If something takes time, we assume it's broken. If we can't see progress, we panic.

But some things can only grow at the speed they grow.

A friendship. A grief. A thought worth keeping.

The rain knows this. It's been practicing for longer than any of us.

I opened the window and let the sound in — not as content, not as ambience, but as company. And for a while, I didn't need anything to be faster than it was.
